[Table S1] tableS1_sensitivity_EN.csv
Title: Sensitivity analysis of mixed-effects logistic regression results across alarm burden time windows
Footnote:
  Results of mixed-effects logistic regression models with bed-level random intercepts, estimated
  using three definitions of alarm burden: the number of clinical alarms in the same ward in the
  preceding 5, 10, or 30 minutes (excluding the index alarm). The outcome is nurse-initiated alarm
  silencing (yes/no). Interaction terms test whether the effect of alarm burden on silencing
  probability differs by priority level. The 30-minute window was selected as the primary analysis
  window based on the lowest AIC (31,653.3) and clinical plausibility (30 minutes approximates a
  typical nursing care cycle). Results were consistent across all time windows: CRISIS alarms showed
  no significant attenuation of silencing response with increasing burden (p = 0.290–0.999), whereas
  WARNING alarms showed a significant negative association in all windows (p = 0.014–0.029),
  supporting the robustness of the primary findings.
  OR = odds ratio; CI = confidence interval; AIC = Akaike information criterion.
  N = 59,113 clinical alarms (July 15 – September 9, 2025).

[Table S2] tableS2_total_burden_EN.csv
Title: Sensitivity analysis using total alarm burden (clinical + technical alarms combined)
Footnote:
  The primary analysis used clinical-only alarm burden (alarm_burden) as the exposure variable,
  defined as the number of clinical alarms in the same ward in the preceding 30 minutes.
  This sensitivity analysis substitutes total alarm burden (alarm_burden_total), which includes
  both clinical and technical alarms. Technical alarms do not produce audible alerts at the
  bedside in this ward setting and therefore do not directly contribute to the auditory cognitive
  load experienced by nursing staff; alarm_burden_total should be interpreted as a ward-level
  workload indicator rather than a measure of perceived alarm noise.
  OR = odds ratio; CI = confidence interval; AIC = Akaike information criterion.
  N = 59,113 clinical alarms (July 15 – September 9, 2025).

[Discussion memo] Total burden sensitivity analysis interpretation
  In the sensitivity analysis using alarm_burden_total, the CRISIS × burden interaction was
  statistically significant and positive (OR = 1.009; p = 0.023), in contrast to the
  primary analysis (OR = 1.000; p = 0.999). This finding is likely attributable to
  confounding by patient acuity: technical alarms in this ward predominantly reflect
  device-related events such as SpO2 probe disconnection, which occur more frequently in
  higher-acuity patients. Consequently, a higher technical alarm count may serve as a proxy
  for a more critically ill patient population, in whom nurses are inherently more vigilant
  and more likely to respond to CRISIS-level alerts. This interpretation is consistent with
  the ward-level observation that technical alarms are perceived as ambient background noise
  rather than actionable stimuli — a phenomenon that further supports the use of
  clinical-only burden as the primary exposure variable in this study.

Title: Sensitivity Analysis: Mixed-effects Logistic Regression Model Including Lagged Alarm Burden (1–2 Hours Prior)
Footnote:
  This model extends the primary analysis (Table 3) by adding alarm burden measured
  1–2 hours prior to each alarm event as an additional covariate, to examine cumulative
  fatigue effects distinct from immediate alarm load. Immediate burden × priority
  interactions and all covariates from the primary model were retained.
  OR = odds ratio; CI = confidence interval. Random intercepts were specified at the
  bed level. AIC = Akaike Information Criterion.
  N = 59,113 clinical alarms (July 15 – September 9, 2025).
